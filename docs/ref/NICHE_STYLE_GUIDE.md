## 3. Brand Voice and Tone

### Narrator Archetype
Charismatic and expert adult male historian -- authoritative yet accessible, painting vivid pictures of the past with confident, measured storytelling. Think a National Geographic narrator who genuinely loves his subject.

### Tonal Characteristics
- **Formality:** 3/10 -- conversational and approachable, not academic
- **Emotionality:** 6/10 -- evocative storytelling that makes you feel the era, without melodrama
- **Humor:** 5/10 -- occasional wry observations, understated wit; never forced jokes
- **Pacing:** Medium -- measured and deliberate, allowing images to breathe, with acceleration at dramatic turning points
- **Viewer address:** "you" -- direct, personal connection

### Tone Examples
- "Amsterdam is a city that should not exist. It sits below sea level, on swampy peat, where storms once erased whole villages. And yet, from this unstable ground came stable ideas: canals that tamed water, banks that tamed risk, and an openness that drew in strangers -- until openness became its own danger."
- "By 1666, London was a city waiting to burn. Timber houses packed so tightly you could lean out one window and shake hands with your neighbor across the street."
- "What you're seeing now didn't exist two hundred years ago. None of it. Just marshland and ambition."

## 4. Vocabulary and Language Constraints

### Key Niche Terms
- reconstruction / AI reconstruction
- evolution (of a city, not biological)
- medieval, Renaissance, Industrial Revolution, Victorian era
- settlement, colony, trading post
- fortification, ramparts, city walls
- cobblestone, timber-framed, Gothic, Baroque
- harbor, port, waterfront
- plague, Great Fire, siege
- urban planning, grid layout, zoning
- documentary, tour, day in the life
- 1700s, 1800s, 1890 AI reconstruction
- skyline, borough, district, quarter

### Prohibited Words and Phrases
- "Hey guys, welcome back" or any generic YouTube greeting (kills the hook)
- "Subscribe" in the first 60 seconds
- Clickbait exaggerations without evidence ("shocking truth", "you won't believe")
- Profanity or crude language
- Modern internet slang (cringe, sus, vibe check) -- breaks historical immersion

### Loanwords and Period Language
Use formally precise historical terms. Professional historical slang is acceptable when it adds authenticity (e.g., "the Bourse" for Amsterdam's exchange, "the Minster" for a cathedral). Foreign place names and historical terms should be used in their original form when well-known (e.g., "Nieuw Amsterdam", "la Cite"). Avoid anachronistic modern jargon when describing historical periods.

## 5. Visual Aesthetic

### Color Palette
- **Primary colors:** Warm taupe #A89278, sandy brown #9C8568, warm umber #A08A6C, cream white #E8DDD0 (for contrast text)
- **Color grading mood:** Luxury warm tones, muted and rich -- like aged parchment and candlelight. Desaturated warmth with golden undertones. Historical footage should feel timeless, not digitally processed.

### Visual Style
Cinematic and historically immersive. Serif fonts for on-screen text -- evoke printed books and historical documents. Heavy use of AI-reconstructed footage showing city construction and transformation with motion effects (pan, slow zoom, parallax). Layered transitions between eras. Visual storytelling that makes the viewer feel transported to the period.

### Visual Prohibitions
- Garish, eye-straining color combinations
- Low-contrast text that's hard to read
- Colors that clash with the warm luxury palette
- Stock photos with modern people in historical context
- Visible AI artifacts on faces or architecture
- Meme inserts or social media screenshots

## 6. Video Structure and Format

### Typical Duration
~17 minutes

### Standard Structure
1. Cold hook (0:00-0:20) -- a striking statement or visual about the city; no greetings
2. Origin story (0:20-3:00) -- what existed before the city; why this location
3. Era-by-era evolution (3:00-14:00) -- chronological progression through major periods, evenly distributed; each era has its own mini-hook and visual identity
4. Modern transformation (14:00-16:00) -- how the city became what it is today
5. Closing reflection + CTA (16:00-17:00) -- what the city's story tells us; subscribe prompt

### Upload Frequency
1 video per week

## 9. Current Video Topic

> Filled per video via `/setup-video`.

**Working title:** Paris Part 1 -- From Lutetia to the Eve of Revolution
**Topic:** The evolution of Paris from its Celtic and Roman origins as Lutetia through the medieval era, the Renaissance, and up to the brink of the French Revolution. How a small island settlement on the Seine grew into one of Europe's most powerful and culturally rich capitals -- through sieges, plagues, cathedral-building, royal ambition, and the slow accumulation of ideas that would eventually ignite revolution.
**Key thesis / insight:** Paris's depth of history -- layer upon layer of destruction, reinvention, and progress -- is what makes it not just a beautiful city, but a living archive of Western civilization.
**Target duration:** ~17 minutes
**Target mood:** Positive and awe-inspiring -- a sense of wonder at how much history is packed into one city, building momentum era by era

### Must-Include Content Points
- The origin of the name "Paris" (from the Parisii tribe) and the Lutetia settlement
- Why this specific location on the Seine was chosen -- the island advantage (Ile de la Cite)
- Roman Lutetia -- baths, arena, roads; what the Romans built and left behind
- Major destructions and sieges (Viking raids, medieval conflicts) and how the city rebuilt each time
- The building of Notre-Dame and what it represented for the city's identity
- How technological and economic progress shaped the city -- trade, universities (the Sorbonne), guilds
- The state of Paris by the late 18th century -- setting the stage for Part 2

### Must-Exclude
- Dry academic recitations of dates and kings without narrative context
- The French Revolution itself (saved for Part 2)
- Modern Paris / post-Revolution content
- Political commentary or modern parallels

### Author Sources
- Wikipedia (Paris history, Lutetia, Ile de la Cite, Notre-Dame de Paris, University of Paris)
- Britannica (Paris, History of Paris, Parisii)
